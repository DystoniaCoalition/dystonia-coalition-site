About us

What is the Dystonia Coalition?

Our mission, how the consortium is structured, and the conditions we study.

The Dystonia Coalition is a research group focused on clinical and translational research in dystonia, working toward better treatments. We bring together investigators, clinical sites, and patient advocacy groups around shared research priorities.

Our history

When the Coalition began in 2009, our focus was on the most common subtypes of dystonia: the adult-onset focal dystonias. Over the following years we expanded to include childhood-onset cases, which are far rarer but often more severe, since they tend to progress to generalized dystonia. More recently, we have also included certain genetic disorders where dystonia is the primary problem — sometimes alongside other symptoms — such as dopa-responsive dystonia and myoclonus-dystonia syndrome.

What we study

The Coalition addresses any condition where dystonia — sustained or intermittent abnormal movements, postures, or both — is the primary problem. Dystonic movements and postures are typically patterned and repetitive, sometimes tremulous or jerky, and are often triggered or worsened by voluntary action.

Because dystonia can look very different from person to person, one useful way to group its many subtypes is by how much of the body is affected:

  • Focal dystonia — one region of the body. Common examples include cervical dystonia (neck), blepharospasm (around the eyes), laryngeal dystonia (voice), and limb dystonia.
  • Segmental dystonia — two or more neighboring regions, such as blepharospasm combined with the mouth region (sometimes called Meige syndrome), or cervical dystonia combined with one arm.
  • Multifocal dystonia — two or more regions that are not close to each other, such as laryngeal dystonia combined with one arm.
  • Generalized dystonia — many regions of the body. This form often begins in childhood and tends to be the most severe.

How the consortium is organized

The Coalition works through a tiered network of sites and an administrative core — described in full on our Meet Our Team page — supported by the researchers, clinicians, and patient advocacy groups who guide our shared priorities.
